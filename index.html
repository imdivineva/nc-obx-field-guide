OBX Field Guide — twelve drafts

Four families to react to: the original "seven zones" editorial concept, the "field passport" checklist concept it evolved into, three drafts that each test one specific fix rather than a full re-skin, and the newest pivot — a literal, narrated road trip instead of a zone taxonomy. Each of the newest three (J, K, L) is a complete concept: a homepage plus both journeys, in its own visual direction.

Original concept — three editorial drafts

Article-style guides you read start to finish.

Draft A

Field Notebook

Warm, paper-and-ink naturalist field guide feel. Serif type, cream palette, hand-annotated touches.
Draft B

Topographic Atlas

Dark chart-room feel — nautical/survey chart aesthetic, monospace labels, grid backdrop.
Draft C

Coastal Journey

Light, photography-forward scrollytelling that drives the route north to south, zone by zone.

New concept — three "field passport" drafts

Not just an article — a tool you'd actually keep open while driving: filter zones by how you'll reach them, log which ones you've stood in, and leave yourself notes. Progress is saved on your device. All three use warm, nature-toned palettes and real photography only, no illustrated maps or diagrams.

Draft D

Field Passport

A dashboard-style checklist — all seven zones as filterable cards, a progress bar, and a "log it" toggle on each.
Draft E

Trail Journal

A personal, keepsake feel — polaroid-style photos, hand-lettered accents, ruled-paper notes, and stamps you collect as you go.
Draft F

Trail Companion

A stop-by-stop trip itinerary — one full-width zone at a time in order, with a trail-progress bar instead of a stat grid.

Newest — three targeted fixes, not full re-skins

None of the six earlier drafts have a real map, a way to identify an unfamiliar zone on the spot, or a version that works with no cell signal. Each of these three tests exactly one fix. They're worth reacting to individually — a favorite idea from one of these could get grafted onto whichever draft above wins out, rather than needing to "win" as a whole draft itself.

Draft G

Trail Map

Fixes: no real map anywhere in D/E/F. A real, pannable OpenStreetMap with pins for each zone's actual location — not an illustration, so it can't read as "AI-generated."
Draft H

Zone Finder

Fixes: every draft assumes you already know which zone you're in. This one asks "what's under your feet?" and figures it out for you in three questions.
Draft I

Print & Go

Fixes: NC-12 has real dead zones for cell service. A compact, print-ready page — check zones off by hand if you saved it before losing signal.

New concept — the road-trip pivot, in three visual directions

A pivot away from the "seven zones" entirely: the site organized as two literal, narrated road trips — "From the Piedmont to the Sea" (Durham to Nags Head, US-64) and "Down the Banks" (Corolla to Ocracoke, NC-12) — plus a homepage with the two large photographic entry points from the brief. Each draft below is a complete concept: its own homepage linking to both journeys. All three share the same underlying content (the 6-question windshield template, the 3-tier photo hierarchy, 15 stops per journey) and differ only in presentation. Maps throughout are real (OpenStreetMap / Esri World Imagery), not illustrated. Photography is stand-in placeholder throughout.

Draft J

Windshield Journal

Extends Draft C's warm, photography-forward scrollytelling — the look you responded well to — rebuilt around the road-trip stop template with a real embedded map per stop.
Draft K

Windshield Documentary

A new, more cinematic direction: full-bleed letterboxed "dashcam" photo frames, dark ink-toned chrome, and a highway-sign-style route strip pinned at the very top.
Draft L

Route Log

Adapts the utilitarian "field passport" family into a mile-by-mile log: tabbed photo tiers, compact scannable Q&A rows, and a "log it" progress tracker saved on your device.
All twelve are self-contained HTML files — open any of them directly, no build step required.
