Every barrier island runs the same seven-zone transect. Learn to read it.

A chart-style guide to the natural environments of the Outer Banks — definitions, boundary markers, and coordinates for standing inside each one.

NASA / ISS · public domain

From orbit the Outer Banks read as a single thin line. On the ground they resolve into seven repeating bands, ocean to sound — this chart plots all seven, plus the sound crossings where you can watch the transition happen from a moving car.

CROSSINGS LOG — NORTH TO SOUTH

What's actually happening beneath the car at each sound crossing, and what's visible from the road on either side of it.

CROSSING
US-158

Wright Memorial Bridge — Currituck Sound

Nearly three miles over water that's almost entirely fresh, with no direct ocean inlet nearby. Watch the marsh grass: pale and brushy here means fresh, not salt.

ON ISLAND

Kitty Hawk → Kill Devil Hills → Nags Head

You're driving the cross-section sideways — beach and dune to the east, marsh to the west, maritime forest and Jockey's Ridge's bare dune field in between.

CROSSING
US-64

Washington Baum Bridge — Roanoke Sound

Narrower and more brackish than Currituck, mixing Roanoke River fresh water with salt water entering from Oregon Inlet. Carries you onto Roanoke Island.

ON ISLAND

Whalebone Junction → Bodie Island

NC-12 turns south. Marsh crowds the road on the sound side almost immediately — less room here for forest to develop between ocean and sound.

CROSSING
NC-12

Marc Basnight Bridge — Oregon Inlet

The one true inlet crossing on this run — ocean and sound water trade places on every tide here. At low tide, watch for exposed pale sand on both sides: the flood- and ebb-tide deltas.

NARROWEST

Pea Island National Wildlife Refuge

Almost no dune buffer — marsh and managed impoundments sit right against the road both sides. Best eye-level marsh view on the drive.

SOUTH END

Rodanthe → Hatteras Island

The island widens; the full sequence returns, ending in real maritime forest at Buxton Woods before NC-12 reaches Hatteras Inlet.

The OBX Explorer is an educational reference to the natural geography of the Outer Banks — not navigational, and not a substitute for official park or refuge guidance. Check current tide charts before entering tidal flats, marsh creeks, or inlet shorelines.
